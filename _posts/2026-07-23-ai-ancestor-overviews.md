Our online search habits are changing rapidly, and the days of trawling through
pages of Google searches are seemingly over. Instead, whenever we type
something into Google, we are immediately met with an AI generative overview of
whatever it is we're looking for. Need to know how much water to add to your
rice? An AI overview will instantly tell you the ratio. Looking for holiday
inspiration, and you want to know where's warm in January? An AI summary will
provide a list of suggestions, all accompanied by a breakdown of flight times
from the UK and average temperature for the time of year. 

AI overviews are even creeping into the world of genealogy, with summaries of
ancestors readily generated---you know the ones, the brief outline of an
individual's life with the key dates, sometimes accompanied by a generic image
or silhouette. I am often impressed---and bemused---at how they make such
little information go so far! This is particularly the case with the ones
produced by Google, where a summary is often the first thing you see whenever
you search for an individual, the caveat being that there must be enough info
available online about them for an overview to appear. Sometimes these can be
helpful---such as when you've overlooked a potential source---but they also
have a habit of muddying the water. 

This was the case when researching Henry Seatle, the nineteenth century curate
of Blawith, Lancashire. Ancestors with clerical roots often leave a paper-trail
–--more so than your average ancestor---and Google can be a great way to find
them. I wasn't sure if anything would come up for Seatle as he was only the
curate of a small, rural township with a fairly modest living. To my surprise,
though, an AI overview popped up with a summary of his life---date of birth,
children, and time served as curate. Most, if not all, could be gleaned from
the records, but one aspect did stick out, and that was his "famous historical
testament" on the working conditions of "pauper apprentices" in a local mill in 1816.

As you can imagine, I was instantly intrigued, and I couldn't wait to track
down the source. Yet when I actually found the source, the reality was rather
underwhelming. It was, in essence, a rather generic statement made by Seatle
confirming that the apprentices attended the parish church on Sundays---not
quite the "notable artefact" in the historiography of child labour promised by
AI. Instead, it was a formulaic statement not too dissimilar to the ones sent
by incumbents up and down the country reassuring their archbishop that all was
well in their parishes---hardly a groundbreaking insight on the health and
welfare of pauper apprentices! 

It is also worth noting that when I did the same Google search a week later, a
different AI generated overview appeared---one which didn't reference his
testimony. Then when I googled him again two weeks later, the summary had
changed once more---this time framing Seatle as an "influential English
clergyman". Whilst I'm sure Seatle probably had achievements which didn't make
it into the records, it would be stretching the truth to suggest he was
influential based on the single source referenced! 

This is not to say the AI summary wasn't useful---after all, it did point me in
the direction of the source in question. Yet it was another reminder to avoid
taking AI overviews at face value, mainly because they have a habit of
exaggerating or overstating the significance of the evidence. It is also worth
playing around with keywords in Google to see the various shapes that the AI
overviews take, and also doing this regularly as AI develops and new sources
are added. A good rule of thumb, though, is to always seek out the original and
verify what AI is telling you---you may well find yourself reaching wildly
different conclusions.
